Hiring Guide (Any Role)

Role: Founder
When: Ready to hire for any position
Last Updated: Feb 22, 2026

Before You Hire

1. Define the Role Clearly

Answer these questions FIRST:

QuestionYour Answer
What problem does this hire solve?
What will they do daily?
What does success look like in 30 days?
What does success look like in 90 days?
Full-time or part-time?
Timezone requirements?
Required experience level?
If you can't answer these clearly, you're not ready to hire.

Role Templates

Virtual Assistant (General)

Tasks: Admin, data entry, scheduling, research, customer support Where to hire: OnlineJobs.ph, Upwork Pay range: $4-8/hr (PH), $15-20/hr (US) Hours: 10-40/week Key traits:
  • Strong written English
  • Reliable (shows up consistently)
  • Follows instructions precisely
  • Asks clarifying questions

Sales Development Rep (SDR) / Setter

Tasks: Cold calls, cold emails, book appointments Where to hire: Upwork, Indeed, referrals Pay range: $15-25/hr base + commission Hours: 20-40/week Key traits:
  • Comfortable on phone
  • Handles rejection well
  • Organized (tracks leads properly)
  • Self-motivated

Closer

Tasks: Demo calls, closing deals Where to hire: Referrals, sales communities Pay range: Commission only (15-25% of first payment) or $20-40/hr + commission Hours: As needed per booked calls Key traits:
  • Sales experience required
  • Can handle objections smoothly
  • Closes without being pushy
  • Understands the product

Fulfillment Specialist

Tasks: Client builds, sub-account setup, QA Where to hire: OnlineJobs.ph, Upwork Pay range: $8-15/hr Hours: 20-40/week Key traits:
  • Tech-savvy (can learn GHL)
  • Detail-oriented (QA matters)
  • Process-follower
  • Communicates blockers

Support Specialist

Tasks: Client questions, troubleshooting, escalations Where to hire: OnlineJobs.ph, Upwork Pay range: $5-10/hr Hours: As needed (can start part-time) Key traits:
  • Patient and friendly
  • Good written communication
  • Knows when to escalate
  • Proactive problem-solver

The Hiring Process

Step 1: Write the Job Post

Structure: 
[ROLE TITLE] - [COMPANY NAME] - [HOURS/WEEK] - [PAY RANGE]


WHO WE ARE:
[2-3 sentences about your company]



WHAT YOU'LL DO:
  • [Task 1]

  • [Task 2]

  • [Task 3]


WHO YOU ARE:
  • [Required trait 1]

  • [Required trait 2]

  • [Required skill]


NICE TO HAVE:
  • [Bonus skill]


HOW TO APPLY:
[Specific instructions - use this to filter]

Filtering trick: Include a specific instruction like: "Start your application with the word 'READY' so I know you read this."

Anyone who doesn't follow instructions → auto-reject.

Step 3: Screen Applications (15 min)

Auto-reject if:
  • Didn't follow application instructions
  • Generic cover letter (clearly copy-pasted)
  • No relevant experience for role
  • Red flags in work history (job-hopping, gaps with no explanation)
Move forward if:
  • Followed instructions
  • Personalized response
  • Relevant experience
  • Clear communication

Step 4: Send Screening Questions

Email 3-5 questions to promising candidates:

Thanks for applying! Before we schedule a call, please answer these questions:


  1. [Role-specific question]
    2. 

  2. [Scenario question: "What would you do if..."]
    3. 

  3. Why are you interested in this role specifically?
    4. 

  4. What are your salary expectations?
    5. 

  5. When can you start?
    6. 


Please respond within 48 hours.
Why this works:
  • Tests written communication
  • Shows if they're serious
  • Filters out people who aren't responsive

Step 5: Interview (20-30 min)

Structure:
  1. Intro (2 min): Quick background on the company
  2. Their background (5 min): "Walk me through your experience"
  3. Role-specific questions (10 min):
- "How would you handle [common scenario]?" - "What tools have you used for [task]?" - "Tell me about a time you [relevant situation]"
  1. Their questions (5 min): What they ask tells you a lot
  2. Next steps (2 min): "We'll get back to you by [date]"
Red flags in interview:
  • Badmouths previous employers
  • Can't give specific examples
  • Doesn't ask any questions
  • Late without notice

Step 6: Test Project (Optional but Recommended)

For VAs: Give a small paid task ($20-50 worth)
  • "Research 20 businesses in [niche] and put them in this spreadsheet"
  • "Write 5 email templates for [scenario]"
For Sales: Role-play a call
  • You play the prospect
  • See how they handle objections
For Tech/Fulfillment: Give a sample task
  • "Set up [specific thing] in this test account"
  • "Document the process as you go"

Step 7: Make the Offer

Hey [Name],


Great news - we'd like to bring you on as [Role]!



Here are the details:
  • Start date: [Date]

  • Hours: [X/week]

  • Pay: [$X/hour or $X/month]

  • Trial period: 2 weeks


If this works for you, please confirm and I'll send over the onboarding materials.



Looking forward to working together!
[Your name]

Always include a trial period. 2 weeks minimum. This protects both of you.

Onboarding Checklist

Day 1

  • Welcome call (30 min)
  • Send all login credentials
  • Share SOPs for their role
  • Set up communication (Slack, etc.)
  • First small task to complete today

Week 1

  • Daily check-in (15 min)
  • Complete training on core tools
  • Handle 2-3 real tasks with supervision
  • Feedback session end of week

Week 2

  • Reduce check-ins to every other day
  • Handle tasks independently
  • Note any gaps in training
  • End-of-trial review

Trial Review Questions

  • Are they meeting expectations?
  • Do they communicate proactively?
  • Are they improving?
  • Would I hire them again knowing what I know now?
If answer to any is "no" → let them go. It won't get better.

Letting Someone Go

When to Fire

  • Consistently missing deadlines
  • Poor quality work after feedback
  • Communication issues
  • Attitude problems
  • Not improving after warnings

How to Do It

"Hey [Name], I appreciate your work but this isn't working out. Today will be your last day. I'll pay you through [date] and send any final payment by [date]."
Keep it short. Don't over-explain. Be respectful but firm.

Getting the Most Out of This Guide

Before Your First Hire

  1. Be clear on what you need (if fuzzy, you're not ready)
  2. Start with a part-time role if unsure
  3. Have SOPs ready for them to follow
  4. Budget for trial period (may not work out)

Common Mistakes

  • Hiring too fast (desperation hire)
  • Not having clear expectations
  • No trial period
  • Skipping the test project
  • Not firing fast enough when it's not working

The Mindset

  • Good hiring is a skill - you'll get better with practice
  • The first hire might not work out - that's normal
  • Paying fair wages attracts better candidates
  • Your time is worth more than saving $2/hr on a hire
